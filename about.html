Hello, I’m Heather Dansie. Born and bred in East London, if I was a bird, I’d be a bright cockney sparrow. My work specialises in cultural and social research and strategy. It is amazing what we can learn if we take the time to look around and listen.

The people and places that inspire us shape who we are. Below are four penny dropping moments that shaped who I am, and the research techniques that I love.

Depth Interviews

This is our family dairy in Shacklewell Lane in Hackney. Painted by my great uncle Colin in the 1950s, I’m amazed and delighted that whoever lives there now still keeps up the paint work. My granddad took me to see his old stomping ground when I was fifteen and that little trip down a road I had always taken for granted was really important for me. It immediately gave me a sense of my roots and I think, a sense of place in the world. My London family are a proper clan, we’ve been in London since at least 1666, we’ve not gone very far and we haven’t done very much, I’m afraid. But the stories are brilliant.

Depth interviews are one of the most powerful way to get to the truth. And it is of course about the recruitment, both the prepared and instinctive questions, but crucially about the power of listening, allowing your speaker the chance to reveal what matters most.

In the 1970s my aunty Jo, who was my best friend and who passed away in 2014, recorded an interview with my great grandma, a truly tough old bird, the oldest surviving member of a family of 17 kids in a one up one down hut basically by the canal. Jo had this incredible ability to just listen. She barely asks any questions at all, and my great grandma completely opens up to her. They talk about the war, periods, the price of butter, everything in this interview. I really do believe it is incredible what people will tell you if you just take the time to listen.

Desk Research

My second penny dropping moment and a place that still inspires me is Oxford. I applied to Oxford because a teacher told me it would be good practice for job applications. At the interview I felt completely overwhelmed and intimidated by the other students; their accents, their clothes, and then the gorgeous city itself with its books and that heavenly dusty smell and the wizened old professors who honestly, come straight out of Hogwarts. But I loved my time there and to this day, my favourite, favourite type of research is desk research. I love trawling at speed through books, articles, images and understanding what collectively they tell us. I believe profoundly in the art of semiotics, and that rather than commissioning something new, believe the answer is usually out there if you take the trouble to look. And I do think this belief stems from the pressure of working at Oxford. My dream office would look like this library.

Ethnography

My third place of inspiration is, well north Africa in general, Damascus specifically. I was 16 when I visited Damascus with my friend for a month. We spoke no Arabic, although we had classes at the local school and we lived with her family in the Palestinian quarter of the city. Travel is often cited as a source of inspiration yet I find it far less stressful to spend time in a new place with a local than to explore alone. I enjoy melting into the background to take it all in. Ethnography of course is the professional name for this love of people watching. I bloody love watching pros haggle. I now have family in Africa yet as I still can’t speak Arabic I just sit quietly, drink tea and watch my nieces and nephews play. Whilst Damascus, Cairo and Marrakech are big cities there are many similarities to London life, but the desert really is another world. It is probably one of the few places where, if you sit still, you can hear absolutely nothing and it is very, very special.

Focus Groups

And lastly, and of course the best inspiration, is my little man. I’ve spent my whole career as a researcher interviewing mums, and it such a strange feeling to have heard other people’s stories and experiences and now feel like I’m living them too. With little ones you see everything with fresh eyes as they are blown away by sand, light switches, even their shadows. And yes I can confirm that the second shift is an absolutely real thing, that sleep is overrated and that the mum judgement is real.

My first ever focus group was with a group of mums. The best groups are of course not when people are telling you the truth but when you can watch them lie and fake it in the social dynamic of the group. In this case, the first mum talked about how she would only buy meat from the butchers for her kids… and there was this truly awkward pause. Eventually another mum hesitantly said whilst she too would opt for butcher meat ideally, she found meat from the ‘upmarket’ supermarkets to be fine. First mum backtracked at Olympian speed to agree with her. In fact she completely agreed, and all the other mums got involved, all lying their socks off. None of them except mum number one had ever stepped foot in a butchers before is my bet, but it didn’t matter. The insight was that you never, ever criticise another mum. And now as I watch the other parents and kids in the playground and chat with my new friends, I can confirm it is true. I know they judge me and likewise I judge them for some of their coping strategies (coping strategies- not decisions!) but we just support and support each other and I do find the parenting experience a fascinating one. Kids certainly make everything harder, but everything better.

So there we are: Dairies and Butchers, Deserts and Libraries have provided me with my greatest inspiration and trained me in the tools of the trade.
